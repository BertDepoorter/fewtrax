Mathematical Background

This page summarises the physics and mathematics underlying the EMRI waveform model implemented in fewtrax. For full derivations see the references at the bottom of this page.

Extreme-Mass-Ratio Inspirals

An extreme-mass-ratio inspiral (EMRI) consists of a stellar-mass compact object (mass \(\mu \sim 1\text{–}100\,M_\odot\)) orbiting a massive black hole (mass \(M \sim 10^5\text{–}10^7\,M_\odot\)) with mass ratio \(\varepsilon \equiv \mu/M \ll 1\). The large mass ratio means

  • the small body follows a geodesic of the Kerr background to leading order,

  • radiation-reaction drives a slow, adiabatic inspiral on a timescale \(\mathcal{T}_{\rm insp} \sim M/\varepsilon \gg M\).

The gravitational radiation emitted during the inspiral lies in the millihertz band detectable by LISA.

Kerr Geodesics

The background spacetime is described by the Kerr metric with mass \(M\) and dimensionless spin \(a \in [0,1)\). Bound equatorial geodesics are characterised by two orbital parameters:

  • \(p\) — the semilatus rectum (in units of \(M\))

  • \(e\) — the eccentricity

The radial turning points are

\[r_{\rm min} = \frac{pM}{1+e}, \qquad r_{\rm max} = \frac{pM}{1-e}.\]

Three fundamental frequencies govern the quasi-periodic motion:

\[\Omega_\phi = \frac{d\Phi_\phi}{dt}, \qquad \Omega_\theta = \frac{d\Phi_\theta}{dt}, \qquad \Omega_r = \frac{d\Phi_r}{dt},\]

where \(\Phi_\phi\), \(\Phi_\theta\), \(\Phi_r\) are the accumulated azimuthal, polar, and radial phases respectively. For equatorial orbits \(\Omega_\theta = \Omega_\phi\).

The separatrix \(p_{\rm sep}(a,e)\) is the boundary between bound and plunging orbits. Inspiral terminates when \(p \to p_{\rm sep}\).

Adiabatic Inspiral

In the adiabatic approximation the orbital parameters evolve according to orbit-averaged energy and angular-momentum flux balance laws:

\[\frac{dp}{dt} = f_p(p, e; a), \qquad \frac{de}{dt} = f_e(p, e; a),\]

where the flux functions \(f_p\) and \(f_e\) are pre-computed from Teukolsky perturbation theory and stored in the HDF5 data file KerrEccEqFluxData.h5.

fewtrax integrates these equations using the Tsit5 explicit Runge–Kutta solver from diffrax with adaptive step-size control. Simultaneously, the three orbital phases \(\Phi_\phi\), \(\Phi_\theta\), \(\Phi_r\) are accumulated. The result is a sparse trajectory of \(N_{\rm sparse}\) points spanning the observation time \(T\).

Teukolsky Mode Amplitudes

The gravitational-wave strain at a detector is expanded in spin-weighted spherical harmonics \({}_{-2}Y_{\ell m}(\theta_S, \phi_S)\):

\[h_+ - i\,h_\times = \frac{\mu}{d_L} \sum_{\ell m k n} A_{\ell m k n}(p, e; a)\; {}_{-2}Y_{\ell m}(\theta_S,\phi_S)\; e^{i\Phi_{\ell m k n}(t)},\]

where

  • \(A_{\ell m k n}\) are the Teukolsky mode amplitudes, tabulated in ZNAmps_l10_m10_n55_DS2Outer.h5 as a function of \((p, e)\) for each harmonic index \((\ell, m, k, n)\),

  • \(d_L\) is the luminosity distance,

  • \(\theta_S, \phi_S\) are the sky angles of the source,

  • the harmonic phase is

    \[\Phi_{\ell m k n}(t) = m\,\Phi_\phi(t) + k\,\Phi_\theta(t) + n\,\Phi_r(t).\]

The harmonic indices satisfy \(\ell \geq 2\), \(|m| \leq \ell\), and \(k,n \in \mathbb{Z}\).

Amplitude Interpolation

Because evaluating the Teukolsky amplitudes at arbitrary \((p, e)\) is expensive, fewtrax pre-computes them on a sparse grid during the ODE integration and then uses B-spline interpolation (via interpax) to reconstruct the amplitudes at arbitrary trajectory points.

Mode Selection

A single EMRI can contribute thousands of \((\ell, m, k, n)\) modes. fewtrax retains only those modes whose relative power exceeds a threshold \(\epsilon_{\rm thresh}\) (controlled by mode_selection_threshold):

\[\frac{|A_{\ell m k n}|^2}{\max_{\ell' m' k' n'} |A_{\ell' m' k' n'}|^2} \geq \epsilon_{\rm thresh}.\]

The default value \(\epsilon_{\rm thresh} = 10^{-5}\) retains all modes that contribute more than \(0.001\%\) of the peak power, giving an excellent balance between accuracy and computational cost.

Waveform Synthesis

Given the sparse trajectory and interpolated amplitudes, fewtrax synthesises the time-domain strain via:

  1. Interpolation of \(A_{\ell m k n}(t)\) and \(\Phi_{\ell m k n}(t)\) from the sparse grid to the full time series at cadence \(\Delta t\).

  2. Coherent summation of all selected modes, projecting onto \(h_+\) and \(h_\times\) via the spin-weighted spherical harmonics and the antenna pattern functions.

The complete two-polarisation strain returned by KerrEccentricEquatorialWaveform is

\[h_+(t) = \text{Re}\!\left[ \frac{\mu}{d_L} \sum_{\ell m k n} A_{\ell m k n}(t)\; {}_{-2}Y_{\ell m}(\theta_S,\phi_S)\; e^{i\Phi_{\ell m k n}(t)} \right],\]

with an analogous expression for \(h_\times\).

JAX Implementation Details

fewtrax leverages the JAX ecosystem for high-performance computing:

  • JIT compilation (jax.jit / equinox.filter_jit) — the full trajectory + summation pipeline is compiled to XLA for CPU/GPU execution.

  • Automatic differentiation (jax.grad, jax.jacfwd) — gradients propagate through the ODE solver (via diffrax’s adjoint methods) and the B-spline interpolation.

  • Vectorisation (jax.vmap) — batched evaluation over populations without explicit loops.

  • 64-bit precision — jax.config.update("jax_enable_x64", True) must be set before importing fewtrax to achieve the phase accuracy required for EMRI parameter estimation.
